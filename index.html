About Me

I am Valeria Villa, a fourth-year Ph.D. student at the California Institute of Technology working with Zachary E. Ross and Robert Clayton. My research focuses on studying regional seismicity, simulating earthquake sequences, and structural seismology.

I work on characterizing the dynamics of seismicity in the San Jacinto Fault Zone in Southern California from a point-process modeling perspective. My work relies on a detailed seismic catalog that I developed using deep-learning algorithms. Building on this work and that of others, I also focus on studying the mechanisms of swarms thought to be driven by natural fluids injected into faults. Additionally, I have a secondary focus on the sedimentary basins underlying mega-cities such as Los Angeles. I developed a method to integrate gravity measurements for identifying P-to-S converted phases in Receiver Functions. The advantage of this method is that it allows for the identification of the interface phases of interest without relying on signal coherence from neighboring stations. Instead, it directly models the gravity data, making it independent of other stations' measurements.

In addition to my research, I create 3D printed models of research outputs such as the Los Angeles Basin depth. I am committed to showcasing the academic results to the general public in an innovative manner.

The Los Angeles Basin

The sedimentary basin underneath the megalopolis of Los Angeles intensifies seismic motions and prolongs the shaking duration. Using seismic and gravity measurements, I discover the basin's depth and shape to best predict the strong motions of large earthquakes such as the potential M8 southern San Andreas earthquake.

The Northern Los Angeles Basins

The structures of the northern Los Angeles basins are important to quantify the seismic hazard of the surrounding megacity, and in particular, the depth of basement is a critical factor in determining the ground shaking levels. In addition, the northern basins provide a mechanism to channel energy from a potential southern San Andreas earthquake into downtown Los Angeles. The Basin Amplification Seismic Investigation (BASIN) project conducted passive seismic surveys of the San Gabriel, Chino, and San Bernardino basins.

Despite their physical proximity, the subsurface structure of the northern basins is quite different in depth and shape, which provides insights about their formation. The San Gabriel Basin is the deepest basin with a maximum modeled depth of 3500 kilometers at the center. Another zone of significant depth is on the eastern edge of the basin. The Chino Basin, generally known as a groundwater basin, is characterized with shallow depths of about 1-1.5 km, and deepen to the north. The San Bernardino Basin, bounded between the San Jacinto fault (SJF) and San Andreas fault, is a semi-circular shaped basin with its deepest section edged closer to the SJF. Other notable depocenter of interest is north of the San Bernardino Basin and east of Chino Basin, which has a significant depth of 3.5 km. The fault locations with the basins have also affected their shape showing possible interactions with their formation.
